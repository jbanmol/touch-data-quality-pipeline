Timing features analyze the temporal patterns of touch interactions, revealing how users pace their movements and whether their timing is consistent or erratic.

📏 Duration Metrics
total_duration Total time from first to last touch event
Duration = last_timestamp - first_timestamp

Why important: Reveals user confidence and task complexity. Quick tasks show confidence, long durations may indicate hesitation or difficulty.

Example: A simple tap: 50ms (confident), Complex drawing: 2.3s (deliberate)

active_duration Time spent actually moving (excluding pauses)
Active Duration = Σ(time_intervals where movement > threshold)

Why important: Distinguishes between thinking time and action time. High ratio of active/total duration indicates decisive action.

pause_duration Total time spent in stationary states
Pause Duration = total_duration - active_duration

Why important: Long pauses may indicate uncertainty, device lag, or careful consideration.

🎵 Rhythm & Consistency
avg_time_between_events Average time interval between consecutive touch events
Avg Interval = Σ(tᵢ₊₁ - tᵢ) / (n-1)

Why important: Reveals device frame rate and user movement rhythm. ~16.7ms indicates 60fps, higher values may show lag or deliberate slow movement.

time_interval_variance Consistency of timing between events
Variance = Σ(intervalᵢ - mean_interval)² / (n-1)

Why important: Low variance = smooth, consistent movement. High variance = jerky or interrupted movement.

timing_regularity Coefficient of variation for time intervals
Regularity = 1 - (std_deviation / mean_interval)

Why important: Values near 1.0 indicate very regular timing, values near 0 indicate erratic timing.

max_time_gap Longest pause between consecutive events
Max Gap = max(tᵢ₊₁ - tᵢ) for all i

Why important: Large gaps may indicate device lag, user hesitation, or interruptions.

time_gap_count Number of significant pauses (>100ms)
Gap Count = count(intervals > 100ms)

Why important: Multiple gaps suggest interrupted or hesitant interaction patterns.

📊 Statistical Timing Measures
time_interval_median Middle value of time intervals (robust to outliers)

Why important: More robust than mean for detecting typical timing patterns when outliers exist.

time_interval_iqr Interquartile range of time intervals
IQR = Q3 - Q1 (75th percentile - 25th percentile)

Why important: Measures spread of timing without being affected by extreme outliers.

timing_entropy Randomness/predictability of timing patterns
Entropy = -Σ(pᵢ × log₂(pᵢ)) where pᵢ = probability of interval bin i

Why important: High entropy = unpredictable timing, Low entropy = regular, predictable patterns.

timing_autocorrelation Self-similarity in timing patterns
Autocorr = correlation(intervals[0:n-1], intervals[1:n])

Why important: Positive values indicate rhythmic patterns, negative values indicate alternating patterns.

event_frequency Events per second (Hz)
Frequency = total_events / total_duration_seconds

Why important: High frequency may indicate rapid movement or high device sampling rate.

timing_stability Consistency of timing over the sequence
Stability = 1 / (1 + coefficient_of_variation)

Why important: Values near 1.0 indicate very stable timing throughout the sequence.

Example: Calculating Velocity

Raw Data Points:
Point 1: (100, 200) at time 1000ms
Point 2: (150, 250) at time 1050ms
Point 3: (200, 300) at time 1100ms
Distance Calculation:
Distance = √[(x₂-x₁)² + (y₂-y₁)²]
Distance₁₋₂ = √[(150-100)² + (250-200)²] = √[2500 + 2500] = 70.7 pixels
Velocity Calculation:
Velocity = Distance / Time
Velocity₁₋₂ = 70.7 pixels / 50ms = 1.41 pixels/ms

Movement features track how users move their fingers across the screen, analyzing speed, direction, and motion patterns to understand user behavior and detect anomalies.

🚀 Velocity & Speed Metrics
velocity Instantaneous movement speed between consecutive points
Velocity = Distance / Time_Interval = √[(x₂-x₁)² + (y₂-y₁)²] / (t₂-t₁)

Why important: Reveals user confidence and device responsiveness. Consistent velocity indicates smooth interaction, while erratic velocity suggests hesitation or technical issues.

Example: Smooth swipe: 200-400 px/s, Hesitant tap: 50-100 px/s

avg_velocity Mean velocity across entire touch sequence
Avg Velocity = Σ(velocities) / number_of_intervals

Why important: Characterizes overall interaction speed. Fast average velocity suggests confident, quick interactions.

max_velocity Peak movement speed in the sequence
Max Velocity = max(velocity_values)

Why important: Extremely high values may indicate sensor glitches or impossible movements.

velocity_variance Consistency of movement speed
Velocity Variance = Σ(velocityᵢ - mean_velocity)² / (n-1)

Why important: Low variance = smooth, consistent movement. High variance = jerky or interrupted motion.

acceleration Rate of velocity change
Acceleration = (velocity₂ - velocity₁) / time_interval

Why important: Reveals movement dynamics - acceleration at start, deceleration at end, or sudden changes indicating corrections.

📐 Direction & Path Analysis
direction_angle Movement direction in radians
Direction = arctan2(y₂-y₁, x₂-x₁)

Why important: Tracks movement direction. Consistent angles indicate straight-line movement, varying angles show curved paths.

direction_change Angular change between consecutive movements
Direction Change = angle₂ - angle₁

Why important: Frequent direction changes may indicate uncertainty, corrections, or complex gestures.

direction_changes_count Number of significant direction changes (>30°)
Count = Σ(|direction_change| > 30°)

Why important: High counts suggest complex paths, hesitation, or course corrections.

path_efficiency Ratio of direct distance to actual path length
Efficiency = straight_line_distance / total_path_length

Why important: Values near 1.0 indicate direct, efficient movement. Lower values suggest wandering or curved paths.

📏 Distance & Coverage Metrics
distance Euclidean distance between consecutive points
Distance = √[(x₂-x₁)² + (y₂-y₁)²]

Why important: Basic building block for all movement analysis. Large jumps may indicate sensor errors.

cumulative_distance Total distance traveled in the sequence
Cumulative Distance = Σ(all_distances)

Why important: Measures gesture complexity and user effort. Long distances may indicate complex tasks or inefficient paths.

avg_distance_per_event Mean distance moved per touch event
Avg Distance = total_distance / number_of_events

Why important: Characterizes movement granularity. Small values suggest fine control, large values indicate broad gestures.

movement_smoothness Inverse of direction change variance
Smoothness = 1 / (1 + direction_change_variance)

Why important: Values near 1.0 indicate very smooth movement, lower values suggest jerky or erratic motion.

Spatial features analyze where touches occur on the screen and how they're distributed, helping identify usage patterns and detect coordinate anomalies.

📦 Bounding Box Analysis
bbox_width Width of the smallest rectangle containing all touches
Width = max(x_coordinates) - min(x_coordinates)

Why important: Indicates horizontal spread of interaction. Wide gestures vs. narrow taps.

bbox_height Height of the bounding rectangle
Height = max(y_coordinates) - min(y_coordinates)

Why important: Shows vertical extent of interaction. Tall gestures vs. horizontal swipes.

bbox_area Area covered by the bounding rectangle
Area = bbox_width × bbox_height

Why important: Measures overall spatial extent. Large areas suggest complex gestures or screen exploration.

bbox_aspect_ratio Width to height ratio of bounding box
Aspect Ratio = bbox_width / bbox_height

Why important: Values > 1 indicate horizontal gestures, < 1 indicate vertical gestures, ≈ 1 indicates square/circular patterns.

🎯 Position & Distribution
centroid_x Average X coordinate (center of mass)
Centroid X = Σ(x_coordinates) / number_of_points

Why important: Shows horizontal center of interaction. Useful for analyzing screen usage patterns.

centroid_y Average Y coordinate (center of mass)
Centroid Y = Σ(y_coordinates) / number_of_points

Why important: Shows vertical center of interaction. Helps identify which screen areas are most used.

spatial_spread_x Standard deviation of X coordinates
Spread X = √[Σ(xᵢ - centroid_x)² / n]

Why important: Measures horizontal dispersion. High values indicate wide-ranging horizontal movement.

spatial_spread_y Standard deviation of Y coordinates
Spread Y = √[Σ(yᵢ - centroid_y)² / n]

Why important: Measures vertical dispersion. High values indicate extensive vertical movement.

🔍 Spatial Quality Metrics
spatial_consistency Measure of coordinate reliability and smoothness
Consistency = 1 / (1 + coordinate_variance + outlier_penalty)

Why important: Values near 1.0 indicate reliable, smooth coordinates. Lower values suggest sensor issues or erratic movement.

coordinate_outliers Number of coordinates outside expected bounds
Outliers = count(coordinates > 3×IQR from median)

Why important: High counts indicate sensor glitches, calibration issues, or impossible coordinates.

spatial_density Number of touch points per unit area
Density = number_of_points / bbox_area

Why important: High density suggests concentrated interaction or fine detail work. Low density indicates broad gestures.

Behavioral features analyze user interaction patterns to understand intent, confidence, and interaction style, providing insights into user psychology and task complexity.

🎭 Interaction Style Analysis
interaction_confidence Measure of user certainty and decisiveness
Confidence = (velocity_consistency × path_efficiency × timing_regularity) / 3

Why important: High confidence indicates experienced users or familiar tasks. Low confidence suggests learning, uncertainty, or difficult tasks.

Example: Expert user: 0.85, New user: 0.35

hesitation_count Number of pauses or slowdowns during interaction
Hesitations = count(velocity_drops > 50% + pauses > 100ms)

Why important: High counts suggest uncertainty, decision points, or interface complexity.

correction_count Number of course corrections or backtracking movements
Corrections = count(direction_changes > 90° + velocity_reversals)

Why important: Frequent corrections indicate targeting difficulties, interface issues, or motor control problems.

exploration_ratio Proportion of exploratory vs. goal-directed movement
Exploration = (total_path_length - direct_distance) / total_path_length

Why important: High ratios suggest search behavior, unfamiliarity, or complex tasks requiring exploration.

⚡ Task Complexity Indicators
task_complexity_score Combined measure of interaction difficulty
Complexity = (duration_factor × path_factor × correction_factor) / 3

Why important: Helps identify which tasks or interface elements are most challenging for users.

cognitive_load_indicator Measure of mental effort required
Cognitive Load = (hesitation_count × pause_duration × correction_count) / sequence_length

Why important: High cognitive load suggests interface complexity or user confusion.

efficiency_score How efficiently the user completed the interaction
Efficiency = (optimal_time × optimal_path) / (actual_time × actual_path)

Why important: Low efficiency may indicate interface problems or user difficulties.

🧠 Intent Recognition
gesture_type_confidence Confidence in gesture classification
Gesture Confidence = pattern_match_score × consistency_score

Why important: High confidence indicates clear, recognizable gestures. Low confidence suggests ambiguous or incomplete gestures.

intent_clarity How clear the user's intended action appears
Intent Clarity = (directness × consistency × completion) / 3

Why important: Clear intent suggests good interface design and user understanding.

behavioral_consistency How consistent the user's behavior is across similar interactions
Consistency = 1 - (variance_across_similar_gestures / mean_gesture_characteristics)

Why important: High consistency indicates learned behavior and interface familiarity.

Raw Data Points:

Velocity = Distance / Time

🎯 Step 2: Quality Assessment - The Report Card System

Think of this like a teacher grading a student's work. We look at multiple aspects of the touch sequence and combine them into a single score from 0-100, where 100 means "perfect touch data" and 0 means "something went very wrong."

🧮 The Quality Score Formula

Sequence Completeness (40% weight)

Does the touch sequence have all the parts it should have?

  • ✅ Starts with "Began" event
  • ✅ Has movement events in the middle
  • ✅ Ends with "Ended" event
  • ✅ Events are in logical order
Temporal Consistency (30% weight)

Does the timing make sense?

  • ⏱️ No huge gaps between events
  • ⏱️ No time traveling (events going backwards)
  • ⏱️ Consistent frame rate
  • ⏱️ Reasonable total duration
Spatial Consistency (30% weight)

Do the movements make physical sense?

🚨 Step 4: Anomaly Detection - Finding the Unusual

Imagine you're a security guard watching people walk through a building. Most people walk normally, but occasionally someone might stumble, run frantically, or move in a strange way. Our anomaly detection works similarly - it spots touch patterns that don't fit the normal behavior.

⚙️

Technical Anomalies

What it means:

Problems with the device, app, or sensors

Examples:
  • Sudden jumps across the screen (sensor glitch)
  • Impossible movement speeds (lag compensation)
  • Coordinates outside screen bounds (calibration issue)
  • Time stamps going backwards (system clock issue)
Real-world analogy:

Like a broken speedometer in a car - the readings don't match reality.

🧠

Behavioral Anomalies

What it means:

Unusual user behavior patterns

Examples:
  • Extremely long pauses mid-gesture
  • Repetitive back-and-forth movements
  • Unusually aggressive or gentle touches
  • Patterns suggesting confusion or frustration
Real-world analogy:

Like someone pacing nervously - the behavior is unusual but not necessarily wrong.

📍

Spatial Anomalies

What it means:

Movement patterns that don't make physical sense

Examples:
  • Teleporting from one corner to another instantly
  • Moving in impossible geometric patterns
  • Touches in areas where no UI elements exist
  • Movements that violate physics (too fast for human fingers)
Real-world analogy:

Like seeing someone walk through a wall - physically impossible.

How We Spot Anomalies: The Isolation Forest Method

Think of this like finding a weird-shaped tree in a forest. Normal trees (normal touch patterns) are similar to each other and hard to separate from the group. Weird trees (anomalies) are easy to isolate because they're so different.

Step 1: Create Random Decision Trees

We build many random decision trees that try to separate touch sequences based on their features.

Step 2: Measure Isolation Difficulty

Normal sequences require many splits to isolate (they're similar to others). Anomalies get isolated quickly (they're very different).

Step 3: Calculate Anomaly Score

Sequences that are easy to isolate get high anomaly scores. Those that are hard to isolate get low scores.

Step 4: Classify Anomaly Type

We look at which features caused the isolation to determine if it's Technical, Behavioral, or Spatial.

🚩 Validation Rules & Flags

Valid Sequence Pattern

Pattern: Began → (Moved/Stationary)* → (optional Canceled) → Ended

Applied Flags for Invalid Sequences

  • missing_Began: Sequence doesn't start with "Began"
  • missing_Ended: Sequence doesn't end with "Ended"
  • sequence_gap: Time gaps > threshold between events
  • short_duration: Sequence duration < 0.01 seconds
  • too_few_points: Sequence has < 3 touch points
  • has_canceled: Sequence contains "Canceled" events

Valid Sequence Pattern

Pattern: B → (M/S)* → E with consistent fingerId

Applied Flags for Invalid Sequences

  • ORPHANED_FINGER: Events without proper sequence context
  • UNTERMINATED: Sequences without proper ending
  • OVERLAPPING_FINGERIDS: Multiple active sequences for same finger
  • PHANTOM_MOVE: Move events without corresponding begin
